Moods






Moods in Turkish decide how the action is done or should be interpreted rather than deciding when it happens(tenses) or who is doing it(conjugation). In Turkish, there are 10 moods, 8 will be mentioned in this section because the other 2 have already been discussed in other pages.
If random capitalized symbols that do not appear in Turkish appear, reload the page.

















What are the moods

In Turkish there are 10 moods which are formed by adding specific markers for the most part. The Imperative and Interrogative moods will not be mentioned because they have been explained in the conjugation page. Here is a list of the moods excluding the ones explained earlier:

  1. Indicative mood

  2. Passive mood

  3. Potential mood

  4. Optative mood

  5. Necessitative mood

  6. Causative mood

  7. Reflexive mood

  8. Conditional mood

Remember that all mood markers must take position 1 in verbs, if you don't know what that means, please go to the markers page.

Indicative Mood

This is the simplest mood, this mood expresses a fact and does not need a marker, this is the mood you'd normally use. However, you can use one to put more emphasis on you thinking that something is a fact.

Examples

T/D⁴r

Almanya Avrupa'da bir ülkedir - Germany is a country in Europe(lit. Germany in Europe a country)
İçerim - I drink
Tavuklar hayvandır - Chickens are animals(Lit. Chickens animal)

Passive Mood

Passive mood translates similarly into English, it's like swapping the positions of the object and the subject(Ex. I see the cat(active) vs The cat was seen by me(passive)). Even though it translates similarly, Turkish does it differently by using a marker.
Now to actually say "by [x]" you need to take the word taraf and add the appropiate personal marker. Optionally, you can add the pronoun followed by the appropiate personal marker for said pronoun attached to it.

Benim tarafımdan - By me
Senin tarafından - By you
Onun tarafından - By him/her/it

Examples

(N)⁴l - Passive mood marker

[x] bir kedi görü - [x] see(s) a cat
([x]) bir kedi görül - A cat is seen (by [x])

(Benim tarafımdan) bir tavuk yenildi - A chicken was eaten (by me)
(Senin tarafından) bir tavuk yenildi - A chicken was eaten (by you)

Potential Mood

Potential mood is used to be polite alot of times, but can cause a "to be able to" meaning aswell. For example: "I can eat." If used with the question marker, then you create a question asking "Can/May/Could I [x]?"

Examples

(Y)²bil - Potential mood marker(ir for aorist is used here)

Yiyebilirim - I can eat
Yiyebilir miyim? - Can I eat?

Tavuk yiyebilirim - I might eat chicken
Tavuğu yiyebilir miyim? - May I eat the chicken?

Optative Mood

Optative moods expresses desires and wants, some call this the "subjunctive mood." Optative is mostly used for first-person, and causes a "Let's..." or "Let me..." meaning.

Examples

(Y)² - Optative mood marker
(Y)²l⁴m - First-person plural optative mood marker

Oynayalım - Let's play
Oynayayım - Let me play

Combine this with Interrogative mood(the question marker) to create a "Shall..." meaning.

Oynayalım mı? - Shall we play?

Necessitative Mood

If you want to say that you need to or must do something, use Necessitative mood. For other people, it comes off more as a "should."

Examples

M²l⁴ - Necessitative mood marker

Gitmeliyim - I have to go/I must go
Gitmelisin - You should go

Hayır, ekmeği yemelisin - No, you should eat the bread

Causative Mood

Causative mood is used to say you made someone or something do something, for example "I made you look" would use causative mood. Causative mood actually has many markers with no real rule on which should be used in which case.

Examples

T/D⁴r / -(²)r / -(⁴)t - Causative mood markers

Yemek - To eat
Yedirmek - To feed(lit. to make eat)

Kediyi yedirim - I feed the cat

Inferential Mood

Inferential mood suggests that what you're saying is based on an inference or is a report on something you were not there to witness. You'd see this mood used on the Turkish news channels. This mood can translate to "apparently," "it is said that," or "must've."

Examples

M⁴ş - Inferential mood marker

O cileği yemiş - He/She/It must've eaten the strawberry

Kedileri ona vermişsin - You apparently gave him/her/it cats / You must've given him/her/it cats.

Conditional Mood

This mood works like an if clause in English.

Examples

S² - Conditional mood markers

Yemeği yerseydim, annem mutlu - If I ate the food, my mother is happy.

Sütü içmezsen, kızarım - If you don't drink the milk, I'm angry.
