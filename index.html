Turkish






Turkish is an interesting language, with unqiue features that most other languages dont have. Turkish is a member of the Turkic Language family, and uses SOV(subject object verb) word order. It is also an agglutinative language, meaning it adds several suffixes to add more meaning or context to a word.

Pronunciation

Turkish pronunciation is pretty easy because of the fact that most letters are pronounced similarly to English, and consistantly is pronounced as such(loan and foriegn words can be an exception). However there are still some challenges such as ü pronunciation and understanding the rules for sound changes. In Turkish, letter consistantly change their sound depending on where in the word it resides. There are Syllable-Initial only sounds and Syllable-Final only sounds. See pronunciations

Word Order

Turkish uses SOV or Subject Object Verb word order(Ex. "I water drink" is SOV word order, versus "I drink water" which is SVO). Verbs should always be last in the sentence, and subjects(especially if it is a pronoun) should always come first.
Below is how words are normally ordered(keep in mind there is a thing called inverted word order, which is used informally by some):

  1. Subject

  2. Time

  3. Place

  4. Indirect Object

  5. Direct Object

  6. Adverb

  7. Verb

(and adjectives come before nouns, like in English)

Vowel Harmony

Vowel harmony is when the first vowel in a suffix(the main way Turkish makes meaning) is changed to work with the last vowel in the base word. There are two types of this, which are easy to do, but possibly hard to remember. Even if it doesn't end in a vowel, the last present in the word matters(Information left out, check the page instead of the overview to find more information).
E-type means there will be two possible vowels: E and A.
I-type means there will be four possible vowels: İ, I, Ü, and U.

In E-Type, the vowel matches the position(front or back) of the final vowel in the base word.
In I-type, the vowel both matches the position and roundness(whether your lips are rounded or not) of the final vowel in the base word.

Conjugation

Turkish conjugation is actually very easy once you understand vowel harmony(even if you don't yet, it's still easier than alot of languages). The endings are always the same, no matter the tense, and tenses do not involve stem changes.

Markers(suffixes)

Markers refer to suffixes, these are the main ways Turkish makes meaning, instead of isolating words. For example: you can add -(y)im to the end of nouns to denote that you possess it, and you can add the marker to a verb to denote that you are preforming that action.

Personal markers
-(y)⁴m/-m - First person singular
-(s)⁴n/-n - Second person plural
-(s)i - Third person singular possessive
-(y)(im)⁴z/-k - First person plural
-(s)⁴n⁴z/-n⁴z - Second person plural
-l²r - Third person plural
